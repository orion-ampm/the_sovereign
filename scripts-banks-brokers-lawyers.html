Scripts for Banks,
Brokers & Lawyers

Words matter — especially when you're asking for what you're entitled to. These scripts help you walk into any financial or legal conversation with clarity, confidence, and the right language.

Women are often talked over, condescended to, or dismissed in financial and legal settings. The right words change the dynamic immediately. Know them before you walk in.
Section 01
Bank conversations
Loans, accounts, rates, and standing your ground
Negotiating a better interest rate
"I've been a customer for [X years] and I'm reviewing all of my financial arrangements. I've received a rate of [X%] from [competitor]. I'd like to discuss whether you can match or improve on that before I make a decision."
You are not asking. You are informing them of a decision you are in the process of making. This shifts the power dynamic entirely.
Requesting your own account after a relationship ends
"I need to open a sole account in my name immediately. I'd also like to understand the process for removing my name from joint accounts, and what documentation I need to provide."
Calm, direct, no explanation needed. You do not owe your bank a reason.
Asking about a loan as a single woman / sole trader
"I'd like to discuss a [type] loan of [amount]. I have [X years] of financial history, a credit score of approximately [X], and supporting documentation including [tax returns / business accounts / pay slips]. What is the process?"
Lead with your evidence. Women who lead with qualifications rather than apology get better outcomes.
If they ask about a partner or co-signer
"Do you have a spouse or partner who could co-sign?"
"My application is on a sole basis. I have the financial profile to support this independently. I'd like to proceed on that basis."
Section 02
Gold broker conversations
Buying, negotiating, and not being taken advantage of
Your first call with a gold dealer
"I'm looking to purchase [1oz coins / X grams of bar] of physical gold. Can you confirm your current spot price, the premium you charge on this product, and your buyback rate? I'm comparing a few dealers before making a decision."
Asking for buyback rate immediately signals you know what you're doing. Dealers give better treatment to buyers who know the terminology.
Pushing back on a high premium
"The premium you've quoted is [X%] above spot. I've seen premiums of [X%] for comparable products from other dealers. Is there any flexibility on your pricing for this quantity?"
Always negotiate. Even a 0.3% improvement on a £5,000 purchase is £15. On £50,000 it's £150. It's always worth asking.
Verifying authenticity before purchase
"Before I confirm this purchase, can you provide the certificate of authenticity, the assay documentation, and confirm the product's LBMA accreditation or mint certification?"
Any legitimate dealer will provide this without hesitation. Hesitation is your answer.
Section 03
Legal conversations
Protecting your assets, understanding your rights, not being dismissed
First meeting with a family law solicitor
"I want to understand my legal position clearly. Specifically: what assets am I entitled to, what is the process for protecting assets I brought into the relationship, and what documentation do you need from me to build the strongest possible case?"
Lead with what you want to know. Lawyers bill by the hour — come prepared and specific.
Asking about a prenuptial or postnuptial agreement
"I'd like to explore a [pre/post]nuptial agreement to protect specific assets. I want to understand what is and isn't enforceable in [jurisdiction], what the process looks like, and what it typically costs."
This is not an unusual request. Any solicitor who makes you feel uncomfortable for asking is not the right solicitor.
When you feel your solicitor isn't listening
"I want to make sure we're aligned. My priority is [specific outcome]. Can you confirm that your current strategy is directed toward that — and if not, explain what would be more effective?"
You are the client. You set the objective. They advise the strategy. Never let this dynamic reverse.
If they suggest you accept less than you believe you're entitled to
"This is the best you're likely to get — I'd advise you to accept."
"I'd like to understand the basis of that assessment before I decide. What evidence supports that conclusion, and what would a more aggressive position look like?"
The woman who knows the language is the woman who gets the outcome. You don't need to know everything. You need to know enough to ask the right questions and refuse to be dismissed.
