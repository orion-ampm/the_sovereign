Asset Protection
Document Checklist

The documents every woman needs — and most never have. Your legal and financial protection, laid out clearly, so nothing can be taken without a fight.

0 of 22 completed0%
Most women don't think about asset protection until they need it. By then, it's too late to put the right structures in place. This checklist is for before the storm. That's when it matters most.
01
Identity & legal foundations
The documents that prove who you are and what you're entitled to
✓
Critical
Valid passport & national ID — copies stored securely
Keep certified copies in a separate location from originals. Store one copy with a trusted person outside your household.
✓
Critical
Birth certificate — original and certified copies
Required for virtually every legal process. If you don't have a copy, order one now from the relevant government registry.
✓
Important
Marriage / divorce certificate (if applicable)
Affects asset division, name changes, inheritance, and pension rights. Know where yours is.
✓
Important
Children's birth certificates stored and accessible
You may need these urgently in family law, medical, or international situations.
02
Financial documents
Everything that proves what you own and what you're owed
✓
Critical
Bank account statements — 3 years minimum
Kept digitally in a secure location. These establish financial history for loans, legal proceedings, and tax purposes.
✓
Critical
Tax returns — 7 years minimum
The standard audit window in most jurisdictions. Keep originals and submitted copies, along with any supporting documents.
✓
Important
Investment and brokerage account statements
Including gold purchase receipts, certificates, and vault storage agreements.
✓
Important
Pension documents and statements
Know what you have in every pension — including any from previous employers. Unclaimed pensions are more common than you think.
✓
Recommended
Business registration and ownership documents
If you own a business: articles of incorporation, shareholder agreements, and annual filings. Know exactly what percentage you own.
03
Property & assets
Proof of what you own — before anyone tries to claim otherwise
✓
Critical
Property deeds / title documents
Know whose name is on every property you have a stake in. If it's not in your name — understand why and what your legal position is.
✓
Important
Vehicle titles and registration
Again — whose name? Jointly held assets complicate separation. Know your position.
✓
Important
Gold and precious metals purchase receipts
Proof of provenance, payment, and ownership. Essential for insurance, estate, and resale. Never discard these.
✓
Recommended
Insurance policies — home, health, life, contents
Know every policy, every expiry date, every exclusion. Store copies digitally and physically.
04
Legal protection documents
The documents that protect you when things fall apart
✓
Critical
A valid, up-to-date will
Without a will, the state decides who gets what. Update it after every major life change: marriage, divorce, birth of a child, significant asset acquisition.
✓
Critical
Power of attorney — financial and medical
Designates who can act on your behalf if you are incapacitated. One of the most important documents you will ever sign.
✓
Important
Beneficiary designations on all accounts
Pensions, life insurance, and some investment accounts pass outside of your will — directly to named beneficiaries. Check and update every one.
✓
Important
Prenuptial or postnuptial agreement (if applicable)
If you have significant assets entering or during a marriage — these documents exist to protect them. Not unromantic. Sovereign.
✓
Recommended
A trusted advisor — solicitor or financial planner — on file
Know who you would call in a crisis. Have their number. Have your documents ready to share. Don't find your lawyer in an emergency.
A woman with her documents in order is a woman who cannot be blindsided. This checklist is not about fear. It is about preparation. There is a difference.

This checklist is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice.
Consult a qualified solicitor or attorney for your specific legal protection needs.
